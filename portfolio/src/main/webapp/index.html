Speedcubing

When I was younger, I always thought of the Rubik's cube as an enigma, easy to pick up, impossible to solve. However, once I set out to teach my self how to solve the Rubik's cube, I realized how simple and logical it really was. A Rubik's cube can be solved in a series of steps called algorithms, which are essentially combinations of moves or turns that must be performed when the cube is aligned or oriented in a certain way. I quickly grew to love it and eventually bought a speedcube, which is made for fast, smooth turns in comparison to the standard Rubik's cube. Once I had learned the basic algorithms and could solve a Rubik's cube within 1.5 minutes, I decided to learn a speedsolving method. There are hundreds of speedsolving methods ranging from bottom-up, top-down, corners-first, edges-first, columns-first, etc. Each has their own unique strenghts and weaknesses. On my next post, I will talk about the Fridrich method, also known as CFOP, one of the most famous bottom-up speedsolving methods.
