Projects

Jetson Nano OpenCV Security

Through a team-based internship sponsored by Duke called Code+, I worked with three other interns to developed a facial recognition application. We used OpenCV and Dlib models as well as a server running on Gunicorn and Flask for our application. A Jeston Nano is essentialy a small, inexpensive Linux machine like a Rasberry Pi that has a built-in GPU and can be run headless. We designed an IOT device from an Nvidia Jetson Nano, a Pi-Cam, and a case to store the components and created a deployable unit. Our program could remotely unlock doors based on whether an authorized person was detected. We harnessed a low-cost, low-powered, embedded solution that could be implemented on the edge of Duke's network. Rather dealing with the latency and security issues associated with sending frame buffers to a server for computation, all computer vision computations were done on-device. We tested on devices such as a Raspberry Pi and Jetson Nano and found that the Jetson Nano was actually more cost-effective given its performance increase per dollar over the Raspberry Pi with a Neural Compute Stick. We made various presentations to stakeholders throughout the project and delivered a final demo at a poster session.

+DataScience in Medicine Research

In a small research team, we harnessed convolutional neural networks and transfer learning to predict the incidence of thyroid cancer and malignancy probability. We worked on finding ways to increase the accuracy and efficiency of our Pytorch models. One method we explored was using the three-dimensionality of cells to our advantage. For each 'slide' in our database of thyroid-cell images, there were multiple versions of that slide at set "depths" in which certain parts of the image would be more in focus than in others. Using this fact, I developed an autofocus algorithm using a gaussian filter and variance to increase algorithm accuracy by using the most "sharpened" depth of a particular thyroid-cell slide, which was then fed into our classifier after it had been trained. We used Duke's massive GPU Computer Cluster to train our models in the interest of time. Throughout this project, we made multiple presentations, culminating in a final poster session.

Speedcubing

When I was younger, I always thought of the Rubik's cube as an enigma, easy to pick up, impossible to solve. However, once I set out to teach my self how to solve the Rubik's cube, I realized how simple and logical it really was. A Rubik's cube can be solved in a series of steps called algorithms, which are essentially combinations of moves or turns that must be performed when the cube is aligned or oriented in a certain way. I quickly grew to love it and eventually bought a speedcube, which is made for fast, smooth turns in comparison to the standard Rubik's cube. Once I had learned the basic algorithms and could solve a Rubik's cube within 1.5 minutes, I decided to learn a speedsolving method. There are hundreds of speedsolving methods ranging from bottom-up, top-down, corners-first, edges-first, columns-first, etc. Each has their own unique strenghts and weaknesses. On my next post, I will talk about the Fridrich method, also known as CFOP, one of the most famous bottom-up speedsolving methods.

CFOP/Fridrich Method

The Fridrich Method is one of the most prolific speedsolving methods for 3x3x3 Rubik's cubes. Also known as CFOP, it is a bottom-up method of speedsolving Rubik's cubes. This method takes advantage of initially setting up a cross (the C in CFOP) on any face of a cube and then procededing to solve the cube with that face facing downward. Throught the entire solve, this face remains facing downward. Once the cross has been formed, when can proceed with the F in CFOP, which actually stands for First Two Layers (F2L). F2L can be performed either intuitively or with algorithms. I generally tend to use a combination of intuition and algorithms when solving F2L as there can be dozens of situations in a given solve. The first two layers of a Rubik's cube are able to be solved simultaneously because you can easily connect a bottom corner and it's associated edge without having to worry about messing up the rest of the solve. Once F2L has been completed, we must orient the the last layer (the top layer of the cube). This is known as OLL and is the O in CFOP. Orienting the last layer consists of two steps: forming a cross on the top, and then using one of a selection of algorithms to get every color on the top the same. If the top of the last layer is yellow, then we want the yellow-face to be completely yellow.Once the last layer has been oriented correctly, we need to permute it. This is known a PLL (Permute Last Layer) or the P in CFOP. Permuting the last layer involves positioning all of the 1x1x1 cubes in the top layer so that they are placed in their correct position. There are a variety of algorithms that can be used depending on how these cubes were oriented previously, and once a single algorithm is performed with the correct orientation, the Rubik's cube is solved. Learning CFOP takes a lot of practice and muscle memory and getting faster involves strategy and learning new algorithms to optimize your average solve time.
